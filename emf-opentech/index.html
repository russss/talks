Electromagnetic Field

Tales from the UK's first large-scale hacker camp.

Russ Garrett — @russss

Hi, I'm Russ. Today I'm going to show you some of my holiday snaps from last summer.

Well, OK, it's a little more complex than that. My holidays don't tend to be very relaxing. And some of my slides are graphs because I love graphs.

Back in 2010 my friend Jonty and I decided that running London Hackspace wasn't really taking up enough of our spare time.

On the continent they have a tradition of running these hacker camps - in Germany they have the Chaos Communication Camp, and the Dutch run a similar event which they enjoy changing the name of to confuse you. The next Dutch event is in July and they're putting 3,500 people in a field for a week.

Anyway, we decided that we didn't want to let the Europeans have all the fun. So, having never been to any of these things, we decided to give it a go in the UK.

So, after a couple of pints in the pub, we decided on an appropriately pun-laden name and Electromagnetic Field was born.

So, what's the point of a hacker camp? It's a cross between a music festival and a tech conference.

A load of geeks in a field for a weekend with talks, a ridiculously fast internet connection, power to the tent, and most importantly booze.

And above everything else this is an entirely non-profit thing.

Of course, we didn't have much experience running festivals, but how hard could it be?

The Site

Well, it seems that the things you think are going to be easy end up being the hardest bits.

In about March of last year we finally decided on a site - a park just outside Milton Keynes. It was pretty well-priced, and the people who own the parks seemed pretty cool.

And in this picture it looks pretty idyllic, doesn't it?

Well, here's the aerial photo and the first thing to note is the excellent transport links...namely the M1 there. Going right through the site. So it was a little bit noisy.

The next thing to note is the sewage works just to the south. Thankfully the wind was blowing from the north for the whole event, because

And lastly, that little stream running through the middle. That's actually the output of the largest system of artificial flood-control lakes in Europe, and I hear they're not afraid to use it. The park floods for 15 days a year.

As you may remember, last summer wasn't the driest on record. I got a bit worried so I wrote a scraper for the river level data on the Environment Agency website...

Networking

One of the main things that people noticed at EMF was that we had rock solid, immensely fast wifi, which is something a lot of conferences seem to have trouble with.

Much of this can be attributed to having a significant proportion of the UK internet industry on our team. If you want an amazing internet connection, you really want the EMF network team on your side...

This is one of the masts we used - it goes up to 30m high and the other one sat in the car park of a datacenter a few miles away.

Once this was finally set up, we were seeing about 380 megabits symmetrical bandwidth on the wireless link, which wasn't bad.
